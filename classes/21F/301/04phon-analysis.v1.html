Review

  1. When a morpheme exhibits allomorphy, its different forms are called allomorphs. The form of the morpheme can also be said to alternate. Sometimes allomorphs are also called alternants.
  2. Under a phonological analysis, we aim to identify the form of the long-term memory representation of pronunciation of the morpheme. This form is often called the underlying form, or the the underlying representation.
  3. A Phonological analysis of allomorphy predicts that the allomorphic variation is systematic. It predicts that morphemes whose underlying forms are the same in a particular phonological aspect, will pattern the same way.
  4. Generally morphological selection analyses does not make this prediction since they effectively memorize which alternant is used in which contexts.

Three Research Questions for Phonology

  1. What is the nature of the mental, abstract, unobservable, underlying representations?
  2. What is the nature of the more physical, concrete, observable, surface representations?
  3. What is the nature of the transformation from underlying forms to surface forms?

How to do a Phonological Analysis

  1. Identify the morphology (the morphemes and how they combine).
  2. Identify the allomorphs/alternants (variation in the pronunciations of roots and affixes).
  3. List the phonological contexts/environments for each alternant.
  4. Make hypotheses about the underlying forms and phonological rules and check them on every data point to see if they work.
    • Generally (but not always), the allomorph which occurs in a variety of contexts or elsewhere is a good candidate for the underlying form.
  5. Repeat the previous step, making changes as needed.

Steps 3 and 4 can be hard!

We will study methods to help us with steps 3 and 4.

  • For step 3, list the phonological environments where each alternant occurs and try to group them according to phonetic properties of the sounds.
  • For step 4, for each proposed underlying form, consider what phonological change will transform it to each alternant in the appropriate context.
  • For step 4, rules and deriviations help us better understand the consequences of each hypothesis and help us see if they “work” for our data.
  • For step 4, we will also compare phonological analyses in case more than one works!
