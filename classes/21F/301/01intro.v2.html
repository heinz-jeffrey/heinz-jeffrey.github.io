What is Phonology?

Phonology is the study of systematic patterns and changes in the pronunciation of words in languages.

Phonological Knowledge is Learned but Not Taught

Example 1

The words below are taken from dictionaries from different languages. Some of these words are English words. Which ones?

ptak thole hlad plast sram mgla vlas flitch dnom rtut

How did you know which ones were English words?

Example 2

Samala is an American Indian language originally spoken around the area of Santa Barbara, California. Samala speakers would recognize the words below as possible Samala words. Note [ʃ] is pronounced [sh] as in `shoe'.

(Begin IPA) K, A, esh, I, N, A, esh, M, U, , A, L, A, M, A, S, P, A, X, A, N, U, S, W, U, N, P, I, , N, I, esh, O, K, esh, O, K, , Q, U, T, I, N, O, W, O, N, , W, A, S, I, S, I, N (End IPA)

They would not recognize the following words as Samala words however.

(Begin IPA) Y, U, Q, esh, U, S, U, , H, E, S, W, A, esh, I, N, , I, S, T, U, X, U, N, A, esh, , M, A, Q, esh, U, M, E, S (End IPA)

Samala speakers are not taught this pattern about their words, but somehow they come to know it. How?

Types of Phonological Knowledge

  • The previous examples were about sound patterns that words in languages conform to.
  • The next examples are about changes in the pronunciations of words.

Example 1: Koasati

Consider the form of the prefix for ‘my’ in Koasati.

Koasati gloss
(Begin IPA) A, M, -, A, P, A, H, T, tie bar, esh, A (End IPA) ‘my shadow’
(Begin IPA) A, M, -, A, S, I, K, T, tie bar, esh, I (End IPA) ‘my muscle’
(Begin IPA) A, M, -, I, L, K, A, N, O (End IPA) ‘my right side
(Begin IPA) A, M, -, I, F, A (End IPA) ‘my dog’
(Begin IPA) A, M, -, A, long mark, P, O (End IPA) ‘my grandmother’
(Begin IPA) A, M, -, I, S, K, I (End IPA) ‘my mother’
(Begin IPA) A, M, -, P, A, T, tie bar, esh, O, K, K, O, long mark, K, A (End IPA) ‘my chair’
(Begin IPA) A, M, -, B, A, J, A, long mark, N, A (End IPA) ‘my stomach’
(Begin IPA) A, N, -, T, O, W, A (End IPA) ‘my onion’
(Begin IPA) A, N, -, T, A, long mark, T, A (End IPA) ‘my father’
(Begin IPA) A, N, -, T, O, N, I (End IPA) ‘my hip’
  1. What are the two forms of the prefix?
  2. When does the [(Begin IPA) -, A, N (End IPA)] form occur?
  3. When does the [(Begin IPA) -, A, M (End IPA)] form occur?

The basic form is [(Begin IPA) -, A, M (End IPA)] with [m] changing to [n] before [t].

Example 2: Georgian

Consider the form of the adjectival suffix from Georgian.

Georgian gloss
(Begin IPA) P, H, I, Z, I, K, -, U, R, I (End IPA) ‘physical’
(Begin IPA) K, I, M, I, -, U, R, I (End IPA) ‘chemical’
(Begin IPA) A, K, T, I, -, U, R, I (End IPA) ‘active’
(Begin IPA) P, H, R, A, N, G, -, U, L, I (End IPA) ‘French’
(Begin IPA) G, E, R, M, A, N, -, U, L, I (End IPA) ‘German’
(Begin IPA) R, E, A, K, T, I, -, U, L, I (End IPA) ‘reactive’
  1. What are the two forms of the suffix?
  2. When does the [-uli] form occur?
  3. When does the [-uri] form occur?

The basic form is [-uri] with [r] changing to [l] if the stem contains [r].

Summary

  • Phonology is the study of systematic patterns and changes in the pronunciation of words in languages.
  • Unlike phonetics, which is concerned with the physical realization and perception of speech, phonology is concerned with the mental aspects of how speech is represented, planned, produced, and perceived.
