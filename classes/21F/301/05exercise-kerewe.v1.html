  1. We assume we have already identified the prefixes for the Infinitive, 1sg habitual, 3sg habitual, and Imperative as [ku-, m-, a-] and zero, respectively. And that ‘adorn’, ‘line up’, ‘measure’, ‘be light’ , ‘make fire with stick’, and ‘be bent’ have only one allomorph whereas ‘hunt’, ‘carry’, ‘create’, ‘guide’, ‘gasp’, and ‘breathe’ have two allomorphs.

  2. To do a phonological analysis then we have to identify the underlying representation of the verbs. When they have one allomorph it is straightforward.

  3. For the other words, there are two obvious alternatives the p-initial form or the h-initial form.

  4. It is useful to list the phonological contexts (also called environments) where each allomorph occurs. The underscore indicates the place where the alternation in the allomorph belongs.

Note we use the "#" symbol to indicate the beginnings and ends of words.

  1. We should endeavor to systematically consider the consequences of each alternative!

  2. Suppose we assume the underlying representation corresponds to the p-initial allomorph. For example, under this scenario, we would say that the underlying representation of ‘hunt’ is /piiga/. What follows from this? Well this correctly derives the 1sg habitual forms but not the others. We would need a phonological rule that converts [p] to [h] in some context. What would that context be? Consulting our list, we might say "after [u]" and "after [a]" and "at the beginnings of words". But that makes wrong predictions for the infinitive of ‘adorn’ and those other verbs!

  3. Suppose we assume the underlying representation corresponds to the h-initial allomorph. For example, under this scenario, we would say that the underlying representation of ‘hunt’ is /hiiga/. What follows from this? Well this correctly derives the infinite, 3sg habitual, and imperative forms but not the 1st singular habitual. There, we would need a phonological rule that converts [h] to [p] in some environment. What is that environment? Well considering the 1st singular habitual form, we might say "after [m]". We check to see if this makes the correct predictions across the entire data set. It does!

  4. We explored two phonological hypotheses and concluded the one with the h-initial underlying representations and the "[h] to [p] after [m]" rule is the better one.

  5. General Lesson: The allomorph that occurs in a specific environment likely has undergone a phonological change. So the allomorph which occurs in a greater variety of phonological contexts is a good candidate for the underlying form.
