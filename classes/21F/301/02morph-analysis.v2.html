Analysis and Synthesis

Analysis breaks things into its pieces:

unbreakable breaks apart into: un - break - able

Synthesis puts pieces together:

-able, un-, break can be put together to form: unbreakable

Morphological Analysis

  • Morphological analysis identifies the individual morphemes within words and phrases.
  • There are two general methods (Bale and Reiss (2018, Unit 6)): Comparison and Subtraction.

Comparison and Subtraction Methods

  • By comparing utterances that share one aspect of meaning, we can isolate the sound associated with that meaning (comparison method).
  • By subtracting known sound sequences from an utterance (while also subtracting the corresponding meaning), we can isolate the sound associated with the residual meaning (subtraction method).

Let’s Practice

Issanoan (not a real language)

Issanoan gloss
a. (Begin IPA) N, A, L, E (End IPA) ‘some hat’
b. (Begin IPA) N, A, L, O (End IPA) ‘the hat’
c. (Begin IPA) P, I, K, E (End IPA) ‘some frog’
  • By comparing (a) and (c), we can conclude that [e] means ‘some.’
  • By comparing (a) and (b), we can conclude that [nal] means ‘hat.’
  • By subtracting [nal] from [nalo] we can conclude that [o] means ‘the.’
  • By subtracting [e] from [pike] we can conclude that [pik] means ‘frog.’

More Practice

Orellan (not a real language)

Orellan gloss
a. (Begin IPA) E, N, A, L (End IPA) ‘some hat’
b. (Begin IPA) O, N, A, L (End IPA) ‘the hat’
c. (Begin IPA) E, P, I, K (End IPA) ‘some frog’

Here, the same methods reveal the definite and indefinite articles to be prefixes, not suffixes.

Zero Morphemes

It is also possible that some meanings are not associated with a form.

Kangaruman (not a real language)

Kangaruman gloss
a. (Begin IPA) N, A, L, E (End IPA) ‘some hat’
b. (Begin IPA) N, A, L (End IPA) ‘the hat’
c. (Begin IPA) P, I, K, E (End IPA) ‘some frog’
d. (Begin IPA) P, I, K (End IPA) ‘the frog’

We often refer to morphemes like the definite article ‘the’ in Kangaruman as zero (null) morphemes (affixes).
