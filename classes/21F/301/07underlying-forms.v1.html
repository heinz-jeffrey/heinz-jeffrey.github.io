Where do we find the underlying forms?

  1. We have already seen that the isolation form is not reliably the underlying form. (E.g. Russian, Finnish, Saami, Serbo-Croatian)
  2. Next we will see that that the underlying form is not reliably found in any particular morphological context.
  3. Finally, we will see that the underlying form may not be found in any single surface form.

taui tauia 'repay' taʔe taʔea 'smash'
sa:ʔili sa:ʔilia 'look for' tafe tafea 'flow'
vaŋai vaŋaia 'face each other' ta:upule ta:upulea 'confer'
paʔi paʔia 'touch' palepale palepalea 'hold firm'
  1. There are two allmorphs of the perfective suffix: [-ia] and [-a]. The [ia] form only occurs before stems which end with back vowels and the [a] form only occurs before stems which end in front vowels.
  2. One phonological analysis is that the underlying form /-ia/ and that the vowel /i/ deletes before front vowels. For example, the perfective form of 'fly' is [lelea] would derive from underlying /leleia/ and the /i/ deletes because it is preceded by a front vowel. The alternative analysis would insert [i] between a back vowel and [a]. This also appears to make the right predictions. For example, the perfective of 'rub' [oloia] would derive from the underlying /oloa/ and the [i] is inserted between the back vowel [o] and the [a].
  3. In either analysis, the underlying form of the verb coincides with its pronunciation in the simple form.

  1. These examples confirm the analysis with /i/ deletion.
  2. But another alternation with zero is present. This allomorphy is best analyzed as belonging to the stem, not to the suffixes. For example, there are two allomorphs of 'stand' [tuː] in the simple form and [tuːl] in the prefective form. Since the consonant varies across verbs, the best phonological analysis would be to posit the consonant as part of the underlying form of the verb and to posit a phonological process deleting word final consonants. Thus the simple form [tuː] 'stand' would be derived from underlying /tːul/.

  3. In these examples, the underlying form of the verb coincides with the pronunciation of the allomorph in the perfective form, not the simple form.

  4. The lesson is that searching for the underlying form is not equal to deciding which column 'contains' it.
