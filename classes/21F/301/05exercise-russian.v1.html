  1. We observe that the nouns with more than one allomorph consistently show an alternation between a voiced and voiceless consonants (specifically stops and fricatives) at the ends of these nouns. The nominative singular form is consistently voiceless.

  1. With two allomorphs there are at least two underlying representations (UR) to consider for each morpheme with more than one allomorph. For example for ‘bread’, we could posit /xlep/ or /xleb/.

  2. Hypothesis: The UR of the nouns with more than one allomorph ends with the voiceless consonant. Under this hypothesis, we need to voice this consonant between two vowels. For example, if the underlying form of ‘bread’ is /xlep/, to its the dative singular form we would combine /xlep/ with /-u/ to get /xlepu/ and then the /p/ would have to voice to give us the pronounced form [xlebu]. We can call this rule "Intervocalic Voicing" and it can be expressed simply as "Stops and fricatives become voiced between two vowels".

    When we check this rule against all the examples however, we see that it fails because it predicts that all nouns which end in a voiceless stop or a voiceless fricative should show the same allomorphy in the data singular form. For example, this hypothesis predicts that the dative singular form of ‘skull’ would be [t͡ʃerebu], when in fact it is [t͡ʃerepu].

  3. Hypothesis: The UR of the nouns with more than one allomorph ends with the voiced consonant. Under this hypothesis, we need to devoice this consonant in some context. For example, if the underlying form of ‘bread’ is /xleb/, to derive the nominative singular form we would combine /xleb/ with the zero affix to get /xleb/ and then the /b/ would have to devoice to give us the pronounced form [xlep]. When we examine the contexts from the above table we see they all have in common that they occur at the ends of words. We can call this rule "Word Final Devoicing" and it can be expressed simply as "Stops and Fricatives devoice at the ends of the words."

    When we check this rule against all the examples, we see that it accounts for every form. Consider how it accounts for the nominative and singular forms of the words ‘threshold’ and ‘vice’ whose underlying forms are /porog/ and /porok/ respectively.

  1. Main Lessons: Check the predictions against all forms. If it makes the wrong predictions in many places, discard it and consider the alternative.

  2. Related Lessons:

  • The isolation form is not always the underlying form! In Russian, the underlying forms is more faithfully represented in the pronunciation of the dative singular, not the nominative singular!

  • If a sound shows different behaviors -- for example sometimes it alternates and sometimes it doesn't -- that is a clue that there are distinct underlying representations. In Russian, some voiceless stops (and fricatives) in nouns become voiced but some don't, such as the [k] in ‘vice’ and ‘threshold’. This indicates that they have different underlying representations. In this case, /k/ and /g/ respectively.

  • The pigeonhole principle applies: if there are distinct underlying representations and one of them is already taken, then the other one must take whatever remaining form is available. In Russian, when a noun shows no alternation, the underlying form has to include the voiceless consonant at the noun's end. So for the nouns that have multiple allomorphs, the hypothesis that they are underlying voiceless would fail to distinguish them from the nouns that don't have multiple allomorphs. So nouns with multiple allomorphs must include the voiceless consonant at the noun's end.
